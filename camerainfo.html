What is a camera?

Imagine a tiny magic box that can steal moments right out of real life and keep
them forever, that’s basically what a camera means to me.

Technically, it’s a device that captures light through a lens and turns what you
see into a photo or video. But the cool part? It’s like a time machine for emotions.
One click can save a laugh, a sunset, or even your dog’s derpy face mid-zoomies. Lol

Cameras aren’t just gadgets, they’re tools for telling stories without words.
And hey, even your phone has one! So we can all be photographers now. :)

How does the camera work?

In a film camera, light passes through the lens and hits the photosensitive film inside the camera.
The film is coated with light-sensitive chemicals that react to the incoming light, capturing an image
as a latent (invisible) image.

Process:
- Exposure: When you take a picture, light hits the film, causing a chemical reaction that records the image.
- Developing: The film is submerged in a series of chemical baths to make the image visible.
- Fixing: A fixer chemical stabilizes the image, preventing further exposure to light.
- Printing: If the film is negative (common in traditional film photography), it is used
to create a positive print on light-sensitive paper.

In digital camera, it works similarly to a film camera but replaces film with an image sensor (either CMOS or CCD)

Light enters the lens— the lens focuses light onto the image sensor.
Image sensor captures the light—- the sensor, made up of millions of
tiny photosites (pixels), converts light into an electrical signal.
Image processing—- the camera's image processor (such as Canon’s DIGIC or Sony’s BIONZ)
processes the raw sensor data, adjusting brightness, contrast, and colour.
Image Storage— the processed image is saved as a JPED, RAW, or other format on a memory card/SD card.

Real-Time Display— unlike film cameras, digital cameras allow you to view the image immediately on the
LCD screen or electronic viewfinder.

When was the first camera ever made?

Joseph Nicephore’s heliograph was the first successful attempt at capturing an image using a camera obscura
and a light-sensitive material, though the process required extremely long exposure times. His most famous
surviving image, View from the Window at Le Gras (1826 or 1827), is the earliest known permanent photograph.

Louis Daguerre built upon Niepce’s work and, in 1839, introduced the daguerreotype process. It significantly
reduced exposure times and produced sharper, more detailed images on silver-plated copper sheets.
The daguerreotype became the first widely adopted photographic technique, marking the true beginning of modern photography.

Why do people not want to be photographed?

I made a survey through Google Forms for my schoolmates' voices. Based on 125 students that answered the survey are:
36.8% of people liked being photographed because most of them are interested in photography and have confidence in
themselves. Meanwhile, 40% of people don't like being photographed because they are self-conscious, uncomfortable,
shy, and sometimes they just prefer being the one getting behind the camera rather than the art themselves. Lastly,
22.4% of people don't mind getting pictures taken because they are more likely to just want to go with the flow and
sometimes it depends on the situations or people who will take their photos.
